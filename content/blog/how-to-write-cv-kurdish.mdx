# How to Write a CV in Kurdish: A Complete Guide for Job Seekers in Kurdistan

You've finished your degree. Maybe you've already had a job or two. Now you're looking for something better — a role that actually fits your skills and ambitions. And the first thing standing between you and that interview? Your CV.

A strong CV doesn't just list what you've done. It tells a story about who you are, what you bring to the table, and why a hiring manager should pick up the phone and call you. In Kurdistan's evolving job market, that matters more than ever.

This guide walks you through everything you need to know — from what to include, to which language to write in, to the mistakes that get CVs thrown in the bin.

---

## Why Your CV Matters in Kurdistan's Job Market

Kurdistan Region of Iraq has seen real economic growth over the past decade. Erbil, Sulaymaniyah, and Duhok are home to international companies, NGOs, government agencies, telecom giants, banks, and a fast-growing private sector. Competition for good jobs is fierce.

Hiring managers often receive dozens — sometimes hundreds — of applications for a single position. Most CVs get 10–15 seconds of attention before a decision is made. If yours doesn't immediately communicate your value, it gets skipped.

A well-structured, professional CV signals that you take yourself seriously. It shows you can organize information, communicate clearly, and that you respect the employer's time. In a market where many candidates still submit messy, outdated documents, a clean CV alone can put you ahead.

## What to Include in Your CV

A solid CV for the Kurdish job market should include these core sections:

### 1. Personal Information
Your full name, phone number, email address, and city. You don't need to include your full home address, age, or a photo unless the employer specifically asks.

### 2. Professional Summary
Two to four sentences at the top of your CV that summarize who you are and what you offer. Think of it as your elevator pitch. Tailor it for each job you apply to.

*Example:* "Civil engineering graduate with 3 years of site supervision experience in residential construction projects across Erbil. Strong technical skills in AutoCAD and project scheduling. Looking to join a mid-size construction firm with regional ambitions."

### 3. Work Experience
List your jobs in reverse chronological order (most recent first). For each role, include:
- Job title
- Company name
- Dates (month/year is enough)
- 3–5 bullet points describing what you actually did and achieved

Use action verbs: managed, designed, coordinated, increased, reduced, trained. Avoid vague descriptions like "responsible for tasks."

### 4. Education
Again, most recent first. Include your degree, institution, and graduation year. If you graduated with honors or completed relevant coursework, mention it.

### 5. Skills
A short list of hard skills (software, languages, tools) and soft skills (leadership, communication). Be honest — don't list skills you can't demonstrate.

### 6. Languages
In Kurdistan, this section is important. List all languages you speak and your proficiency level: basic, conversational, professional, or native.

### 7. Certifications & Training (optional but valuable)
Any courses, certificates, or workshops you've completed. Even short online courses from platforms like Coursera or Google count if they're relevant.

## Choosing the Right Language: Kurdish, Arabic, or English?

This is one of the most common questions — and the answer depends on the job.

**Write in Kurdish (Sorani or Kurmanji)** when:
- Applying to local government positions
- The company or organization primarily operates in Kurdish
- The job posting itself is in Kurdish

**Write in Arabic** when:
- Applying to Iraqi federal institutions or companies based in Baghdad
- The job ad is in Arabic

**Write in English** when:
- Applying to international companies, NGOs, or embassies
- The role involves working in English
- You're targeting a global audience

**The safest approach for most private sector jobs in the Kurdistan Region?** English or bilingual (Kurdish + English). Many HR departments in Erbil and Sulaymaniyah prefer English CVs because they're easier to forward to international partners.

If you're unsure, check the language of the job posting and match it.

## Common CV Mistakes to Avoid

These are the mistakes that cost candidates interviews:

**1. Using a generic, one-size-fits-all CV**
Sending the same CV to every job is one of the biggest mistakes. Tailor your professional summary and highlight the skills most relevant to each specific role.

**2. Writing too much (or too little)**
For most candidates with under 10 years of experience, one page is ideal. Two pages is acceptable if you have substantial experience. Avoid three-page CVs — they rarely get read in full.

**3. Unprofessional email addresses**
`coolkid2002@gmail.com` doesn't inspire confidence. Create a professional email: `firstname.lastname@gmail.com`.

**4. Lying or exaggerating**
Kurdistan's professional world is smaller than it looks. People know each other. Exaggerating your skills or experience will catch up with you — either in the interview or on the job.

**5. No numbers or results**
"Managed a team" is weak. "Managed a team of 8 engineers and delivered a project 2 weeks ahead of schedule" is strong. Wherever possible, quantify your achievements.

**6. Poor formatting**
Inconsistent fonts, weird spacing, and cluttered layouts make CVs hard to read. Keep it clean, consistent, and easy to scan.

**7. Forgetting to proofread**
Spelling mistakes and grammar errors signal carelessness. Read your CV out loud before sending it. Better yet, have someone else read it too.
